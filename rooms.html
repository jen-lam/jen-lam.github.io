UNSW Rooms —

A mobile application designed to supplement and improve the current room booking experience for university students.

Role: Research, Ideation, Design, Prototyping
Duration: April 2018 (2 weeks) - updated December 2018

Overview

Background

During breaks, many students visit the library for an undisrupted place to study, hold groups meetings or interviews. As a result, some students book rooms or media booths days in advance.

As a student who has both booked a room and walked around tirelessly trying to find a place to study, I wondered how the room booking experience could be improved.


Problem

The current booking system causes an underutilisation of rooms and media booths, and does not accommodate students with unscheduled breaks or meetings. These students find themselves walking around each level looking for an appropriate space, which can be quite time consuming.
Challenge

How might we create a simple and efficient mobile booking system for students that will improve the utilisation of time and study spaces?

Solution

UNSW Rooms is a mobile application that allows students to quickly search, filter and view rooms and media booths that better align with their needs. Students are also able to share larger capacity rooms with other students, to improve utilisation.
Research & Analysis

Validating the hypothesis

Student Insights

Before conducting any interviews, I decided to take a step back and observe how students in general go about their day at university. There were students constantly walking - getting to lectures, tutorials, the library, the food court - everywhere. There were students who sat in the library for more than 1 hour and students who were walking around the library, taking a peek through every room door. Very rarely did I see students who were walking around stop in their paths, take out their laptops, and book a room. But what I noticed is that a lot of students tend to carry their phones in their hands when they walk around.

Bearing these observations in mind, I conducted informal user interviews to develop insights into the room booking experience. I asked questions regarding their visits to the library, the room booking experience (if they had any) and observed how they would make a booking. After interviewing multiple students from different stages and faculties, I collated the information to derive the following insights:

Students whose classes are typically at lower campus feel less inclined to study or hold meetings in the library (upper campus)
Some students had started using an empty room only for the people who actually booked the room to come
Students were willing to share greater capacity rooms with other students as long as they are not disruptive
Students have found that rooms that could seat 4 or more people are rarely ever at full capacity
Some students have spent more than 20 minutes trying to find a vacant room on multiple occasion
Many students hope to find a vacant room without having to book one

Current System

To further understand the room booking experience, I analysed the current system and identified possible problem areas. I also noted down the features that could be transferrable to the mobile application format.

1. Use of space
While the layout of the website is clean and simple, a lot of unnecessary space is used up for the 10 PM - 6 AM time slots which appear to never be available (likely due to opening hours).

2. Tedious
To find the most suitable room, students need to consider the amount of people, the equipment needed, and the time slot. As there are no filter options, students are required to click through each tab and hover over each time slot to check the equipment - their efforts may end up being futile.

3. The legend
The legend appears on the landing page, but then cannot be seen straight away after logging in. When interviewing other students, I found that most did not keep that legend in mind and had to find the legend again to discern the difference between a book and unbooked room.

Usability?

As I continued working on the design, I found the positioning of important buttons to be quite challenging. Many mobile applications place buttons in a logical order - with the most important being at the top. However, with the growth of mobile screens and considering the immediate needs of students, I thought that the 'Make a booking' and 'View available rooms' buttons could work better being at the bottom of the screen as they were more accessible.

To decide which design would be more favourable, I asked some of the students I interviewed, along with my friends how they hold and interact with their phones. I found that -

When typing:

  • 80% used both hands
  • 10% used one hand but used swipe to type
  • 10% used a combination of one or two hands, depending on how much they needed to type

When comfortably touching approximately 50% of the screen:

  • 70% would hold their phones with their dominant hand and use that hand's thumb
  • 30% would either hold their phone with both hands or use one hand to interact with the screen and the other holding the phone

And lastly, to reach the upper region of the phone, all students had to reposition either the phone or their hand.

High-fidelity

Prototype

Following a few changes to the initial design and many sketches, the following screens were created to represent the most important functional requirements of the application.

The most notable changes I made were the positioning of buttons and the search field to improve usability.

I want to make a booking

Students are able to book a room or media booth tailored to their needs. The booking form contains default values - the current date and hour (changes after the 15 minute period), a minimum of 2 people, and the main library. These default values make it especially easier for students who are booking on the day.

The system then displays results that match the form input. It also suggests similar rooms that will be useful in the event that the most suitable rooms are all booked.

I want to view available rooms

Ideal for students who do not have specific needs, trying to find a room last minute, or want to check if a room has not been booked. Students are able to quickly check if a room has been booked by searching up the room number. If a student allows room sharing, the room (when searched) will appear as available with a note indicating it is a share room.

I want to check my reservations

Perhaps a student has forgotten which room they have booked for a future date, or they want to cancel a booking.
Reflection

Future Improvements & Key Learnings

Where to next

I believe UNSW Rooms has much more potential to be a better application, bringing an even better user experience for students. Future steps and improvements would include:

  • Testing & feedback - while I did receive user feedback at different stages of the process, having more users testing the prototype would result in more feedback that could be used to create an even better experience
  • Prototyping - the software I used to create the prototypes felt quite limiting and did not truly represent the transitions and micro-interactions that would make the application better. A more sophisticated software may assist in creating them.
  • Features - an opportunity I mentioned that I did not implement was attaching or displaying floor plans to help students find rooms faster. In addition, expanding the room booking experience to all study rooms on campus may also be beneficial.


Key takeaway

Research shouldn't stop at the first step. Allow yourself to be flexible and take on research opportunities that arise during other steps.
