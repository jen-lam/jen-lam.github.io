Work About

Huli System

Background
For a business analysis course, we were tasked with improving the Huli information system and mobile application used by the government-run Karnataka Forest Deparment.

Team: Irene D., Shitang J., Jen L., Ryan L., Nicholas T.
Role: Research, Ideation, Prototyping
Duration: August - October 2018

Problem

The current system lacks integrated business processes, has inefficient data recording and sharing methods, and does not provide meaningful insights and engagement, all of which hinder the efforts towards conserving the forests of Karnataka, India.


Challenge

How might we incorporate recent technologies and develop value-creating features for the Huli system to assist forest guards in the conservation of forests?


Solution

1. Interface redesign
Through redesigning the mobile interface, forest guards are likely to find it easier and quicker to input more accurate data that will be useful to the wildlife conservation deparment.

2. Analytics
A simple, interactive dashboard that allows forest guards to view their sightings history and progress to increase motivation.

3. Integration of instant messaging
By integrating a messaging feature in the current mobile application, forest guards are able to access and discuss reports more readily.

Implementation Approach

To properly approach this project, we first had to decide which project implementation approach we would take. After reading through the project scenario, we decided our approach according to the following:
  • Clarity of user requirements: High - based on the project scenario, we knew the users and processes in details
  • Familiarity with technology: Medium - while we had screenshots of the current mobile application, we were unable to interact with the actual application
  • System complexity: Low - the mobile application served a limited amount of processes
  • System reliability: Medium - should be accurate and expedient but does not need constant uptime
  • Short-time schedules: High - we had approximately 10 weeks to complete the project proposal
  • Schedule visibility: High - as a government department system, required transparency in documentation

We examined three approaches - Unified Process, Agile Scrum, and Design Thinking - and came to the conclusion that the Design Thinking approach would be most appropriate. Despite the need for extensive documentation, we found that the Design Thinking approach generated the greatest amount of value for users, which was our goal.

Empathise

At the beginning of our project, it was imperative that we learned about the users of the Huli information system and mobile application - the forest guards, chief conservators, and the team at headquarters. Our understanding of Huli's users were limited to screenshots of the mobile application, the project scenario, which essentially detailed a day in the lives of a Forest Conservator, and a video explaining the Huli software. Ideally, we would have used these resources to guide multiple interviews with Huli's users but as we were not in contact with them, we interviewed our Lecturer-in-Charge, Sandeep Mysore, who had first-hand experience with the routes.

Interviews + Roleplay
Through interviewing Sandeep, we were able to better understand what the forest guards do. We asked how routes were chosen, who had a mobile phone and access to the application, how reliable Internet connectivity was (if any), and how familiar the forest guards were with technology. This allowed us to either confirm or remove any assumptions we previously had at the beginning of this project. Bearing these answers in mind, we used roleplay to understand the perspective of forest guards. In particular, we roleplayed the patrol routine to identfiy user touch points, and potential opportunities to streamline these processes.
